Eclectus Parrots

Nature's most striking dimorphic parrot — a jewel of living art and gentle companionship

Eclectus roratus

About the Eclectus

The Eclectus is one of the most visually stunning members of the parrot family — and one of the most unusual. Males are a brilliant emerald green with a distinctive candy-corn beak; females are a dramatic scarlet red and royal blue. The sexual dimorphism is so pronounced that the two sexes were classified as separate species for years.

Beyond their extraordinary appearance, Eclectus parrots are gentle, observant, and highly intelligent. They tend to be less demanding in terms of direct physical contact than Cockatoos or Macaws, making them better suited to owners who want a deeply bonded companion without the constant-touch requirements of some other species.

Eclectus have unique dietary requirements that differ from most other parrots — a high-fruit, high-vegetable diet with minimal seed is essential to their health and vibrancy. Our breeders provide full dietary guidance to every new Eclectus family.

Caring for an Eclectus

09

Unique Dietary Needs

Eclectus have a notably long digestive tract compared to other parrots and thrive on a diet heavily weighted toward fresh fruits and vegetables (50–70% of diet). High-quality pellets should be used cautiously — excessive artificial vitamins and dyes can cause health issues. Seeds and nuts should be offered only as treats.

01

Housing

Eclectus require a minimum cage of 36"×24"×48". They appreciate vertical space and enjoy climbing. Unlike dust-producing Cockatoos, Eclectus produce very little dander — making them a better option for households with mild bird allergies.

🎭

Gender Differences

Male and female Eclectus have distinct personality profiles. Males are generally calmer, more laid-back, and eager to please. Females tend to be more independent, assertive, and occasionally territorial — particularly during hormonal periods. Understanding these differences helps set appropriate expectations.

03

Enrichment

Eclectus enjoy foraging activities, puzzle feeders, and shredding natural materials. They are less destructively inclined than Macaws but still require 2–3 hours of out-of-cage enrichment time daily. They respond particularly well to training and enjoy learning routines.

10

Light & Environment

Eclectus benefit greatly from natural light or high-quality full-spectrum lighting. Without adequate UV exposure, their brilliant plumage can lose vibrancy and their Vitamin D synthesis may be impaired. A bright, natural light environment is essential to their wellbeing.

11

Bonding Style

Eclectus tend to bond strongly with one or two primary caregivers and can be reserved with strangers. Early socialization with multiple people helps broaden their comfort zone. They are thoughtful, observant birds — they will watch and assess before engaging, which is simply their nature, not shyness.
